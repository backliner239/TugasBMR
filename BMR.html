Chronicle of the Malay World

The Complete History of
Melayu Riau.

From the Buddhist empire of Srivijaya to the declaration of Indonesian independence — a five-century chronicle of Malay sovereignty, faith, and maritime power.

7th – 13th Century

The Age of Srivijaya

Long before any sultanate, the Riau region lay within the orbit of Srivijaya — a powerful Buddhist thalassocracy centred in Sumatra. Evidence of this civilisation survives at Muara Takus, a Buddhist temple complex in present-day Kampar Regency, dated to the 11th–12th century.

Following Srivijaya's decline, the Riau Islands came under the Bentan Kingdom around 1100 AD. According to the Sejarah Melayu — the earliest text to discuss the Malay world, compiled by Tun Sri Lanang in 1612 — the legendary Sang Nila Utama was king of Bintan before establishing the settlement that would become Singapore.

Key Fact

"The Malay kingdom started from the 12th-century Bintan-Tumasik Kingdom, followed by a period of Islamic Malay Sultanates."

— Riau Malays, Wikipedia
15th Century

Malacca & The Coming of Islam

The Riau Archipelago became part of the Malaccan Sultanate after the expansion led by Tun Perak in the 15th century. Malacca was the golden crucible of Malay-Islamic civilisation: its ruler bore the title Sultan and served simultaneously as head of state and guardian of Islam.

Through Malacca, Islam spread throughout the entire Malay Peninsula, Riau Islands, Bintan, Lingga, and eastern Sumatra — including Jambi, Bengkalis, Siak, Rokan, Indragiri, Kampar, and Kuantan. Malacca also acted as a catalyst for Islamisation as far as Palembang, Brunei, North Borneo, the Sulu Islands, and Mindanao.

The legal framework of this era — the Qanun Melaka — wove Islamic jurisprudence into political administration, creating the cultural blueprint that all subsequent Malay sultanates in Riau would inherit.

Name Origin

The word "Riau" has three proposed origins: from the Portuguese rio (river), from the Arabic riahi (sea water, via One Thousand and One Nights), or from the Malay riuh meaning "crowded with working people" — reflecting the bustling trade entrepôt that flourished after Raja Kecil moved the kingdom's centre to Ulu Riau in 1719.

1641 – 1722

The Johor-Riau Empire

When the Portuguese captured Malacca in 1511, the Malay court retreated south, eventually forming the Kingdom of Johor in 1641 — rebuilt with Dutch assistance after expelling the Portuguese. The cities along the Johor and Riau rivers soon became the heartbeat of regional trade.

On 27 September 1673, the Laksamana (Admiral) of Johor, Tun Abdul Jamil, founded a settlement at Sungai Carang, Ulu Riau, on Bintan Island — later known as Riau Lama. What began as a fortress became a major entrepôt. By 1722, Sultan Ibrahim had relocated the capital of the entire Johor empire here, where it would remain for 65 years.

During the early 18th century, Bugis warriors became indispensable political partners of the Malay sultans. The Persetiaan Sungai Baru (Oath of Sungai Baru) formalised this alliance: by adat, only Malays could hold the title of Sultan, while the position of Yang di-Pertuan Muda was reserved exclusively for the Bugis. This oath was renewed five times between 1722 and 1858.

The Bugis-Malay Alliance

The adat called for a clear separation of powers sworn between the Malay and Bugis houses. Under this order, Riau became the heart of Bugis political influence across the western Malay World — a dual sovereignty that preserved Malay culture while harnessing Bugis martial and mercantile strength.

1722 – 1949

The Siak Sri Indrapura Sultanate

Founded in 1722 by Sultan Abdul Jalil Rahmat Shah — known popularly as Raja Kecil — after he failed to seize the Johor throne, the Siak Sultanate rose to become the dominant Malay Islamic power on eastern Sumatra. Its wealth derived from control of timber, camphor, benzoin, tin, and gold trading through the Malacca Strait.

The fifth sultan, Muhammad Ali Abdul Jalil Muazzam Shah (r. 1780–1782), relocated the government to Senapelan — the city now known as Pekanbaru, the capital of Riau Province today. He is celebrated as the founder of Pekanbaru and posthumously styled Marhum Pekan.

The seventh sultan, Ali Abdul Jalil Syaifuddin Ba'alawi, was the first of Arab (Sayyid) descent. Under his reign the kingdom reached its peak. The Siak Sultanate briefly extended influence even into the Minangkabau Plateau of western Sumatra, before the Padri War constrained it.

After signing a treaty of suzerainty with the Dutch in 1858 (and a further reducing treaty in 1873), the sultanate continued as an indirectly ruled state. Its magnificent Istana Siak — built from 1889 during the reign of Sultan Syarif Hasyim — reflects this complex heritage, blending Malay, Arabic, and European architectural styles.

1824 – 1911

The Riau-Lingga Sultanate

Following the death of Sultan Mahmud III of Johor and the succession crisis that followed, the Anglo-Dutch Treaty of 1824 formally partitioned the Johor-Riau empire. Peninsular Johor and Singapore passed to British influence; the Riau Archipelago and Lingga Islands became the Riau-Lingga Sultanate under Dutch suzerainty.

The first sultan, Abdul Rahman, was crowned as the sultanate's founding ruler. His historical territory corresponds almost exactly to present-day Riau Islands Province, Indonesia. The sultanate governed vital sea lanes and prospered through trade in tin from Billiton, gambier for dyeing, pepper, and spices.

The island of Penyengat became a celebrated intellectual centre. Raja Ali Haji — a Bugis-Malay scholar of immense stature — championed the preservation of Malay language and culture from the palace court. He stressed that abandoning Malay traditions would destroy social order, while borrowing Western or Chinese customs amounted to a betrayal of Malayness.

The sultanate was also known for its Malay gamelan (gamelan Melayu), joget gamelan dance, and the printing presses of Al-Ahmadiah and Riauwiyah — all publishing in Jawi script to preserve Islamic and cultural knowledge. On 3 February 1911, Dutch authorities forcibly dissolved the sultanate.

Raja Ali Haji's Teaching

"Abandoning Malay traditions would destroy the world order and the Kerajaan, while adopting Western dress would be tantamount to betrayal of Malayness."

— Raja Ali Haji, Penyengat Island, c. 1860

The 1784 Battle

On 6 January 1784, the Johor-Riau forces under Haji Fisabillah triumphed against the Dutch East India Company at Tanjung Pinang — one of the most celebrated Malay military victories against colonial power.

1824 – 1945

Culture Under Colonial Rule

Despite Dutch suzerainty, Malay culture and Islamic identity deepened rather than diminished. Integration of Islam into Malay custom became a defining characteristic: by Malay convention, a non-Muslim is not considered Malay. The Malay language — Bahasa Melayu — served as the official tongue of the sultanates and as the lingua franca of the entire archipelago.

The Siak Sultanate signed key treaties with the Dutch in 1858 and 1873. The Dutch established a Residency covering Siak and the Deli regions in 1873. Despite political restriction, the sultans continued to patronise court arts, traditional dress (Baju Melayu, Baju Kurung, the ceremonial Tanjak Melayu headscarf), and the keris — a dagger of profound symbolic and spiritual significance worn by Malay warriors.

Riau's multicultural population — Malay, Javanese, Chinese, Minangkabau, Batak, Bugis, and Banjarese — coexisted within a framework where Malay culture, as defined by language, Islam, and adat, served as the province's unifying identity. The four aspects of Riau Malay custom — adat sebenar adat, adat yang diadatkan, adat yang teradat, and Adat Istiadat — governed all aspects of social life.

1945 – Present

Union with Indonesia & Living Heritage

In November 1945, Sultan Syarif Kasim II — the last Sultan of Siak — sent a cable to President Sukarno declaring his allegiance to the Republic of Indonesia, donating his personal wealth to the independence cause and persuading neighbouring sultans to do likewise. Riau Province was formally inaugurated on 10 August 1957.

The post-independence period saw the dissolution of the Riau-Lingga Sultanate's exile movement: royalist factions such as the Persatoean Melayu Riouw Sedjati (PMRS) had hoped for restoration, but the tide of nationalism carried Riau into a democratic republic.

Today, Siak Sri Indrapura is one of 57 Heritage Cities in Indonesia. The Istana Siak — housing a gilded throne chair, royal crown, royal umbrella, spears, and the bronze statue of Queen Wilhelmina — functions as a museum. Twelve Siak Sultanate buildings are designated national heritage by the Ministry of Culture and Tourism. Riau's government vision explicitly frames Melayu culture as the identity and soul of the province, driving its sustainable heritage tourism programs to this day.
