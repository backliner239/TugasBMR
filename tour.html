Landmarks of Sovereignty and Faith

Royal Residence

Istana Siak
Sri Indrapura.

Built in 1889 by Sultan Syarif Hasyim Abdul Jalil Syaifuddin, the Siak Palace, also known as Istana Asserayah Hasyimiah, represents the apex of Siak Sultanate wealth and influence. The architecture is a breathtaking fusion of Malay, Arabic, and European styles, symbolizing the sultanate's cosmopolitan maritime connections.

Historical Artefacts

  • • The Komet: A rare 19th-century German gramophone, one of only two in the world (the other owned by Beethoven).
  • • Crystal Mirrors: Gifts from the Ottoman Empire framing the royal court.
  • • Golden Throne: The seat of the 12th and last Sultan, Syarif Kasim II.

Source: Perpustakaan Nasional RI & KITLV Archives

Mahligai Stupa

Ancient Srivijaya

Muara Takus
Temple Complex.

Long before the Islamic Sultanates, Riau was a vital node in the Buddhist Srivijayan Empire. Located in the Kampar Regency, Muara Takus is the largest and best-preserved ancient Buddhist temple complex in Sumatra, dating back to the 11th century.

Architectural Significance

Constructed from sandstone, river stone, and brick, the complex features a distinct towering stupa (Candi Mahligai) shaped remarkably like a phallus or lotus bud—a design rarely seen elsewhere in Indonesia. It stands as a testament to the maritime trade and religious syncretism that flowed along the Kampar River.

Source: National Archaeological Research Centre
